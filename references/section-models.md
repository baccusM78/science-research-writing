# Section Models

Use these as generic provisional models. Adjust them against target articles
whenever the user provides target examples.

## Introduction

Function: move from broad field relevance to the specific study.

Generic model:

1. Establish importance of the topic or field.
2. Provide essential background facts.
3. Present the current research focus or general problem area.
4. Map previous/current research and contributions.
5. Locate a gap, problem, motivation, hypothesis, or research opportunity.
6. Describe the present paper, often including aims, method, main result, or
   contribution.

Checks:

- Do not over-review literature that does not lead to the gap.
- Do not give Methods or Results detail too early unless target articles do so.
- End by making the reader ready to enter Methods/Results.

## Methods

Function: make what was done credible, reproducible, and professionally justified.

Generic model:

1. Provide method overview or statement; optionally restate aim/gap.
2. Identify materials, equipment, source, setting, or dataset.
3. Provide procedural detail such as sequence, temperature, parameters, controls,
   replicates, software, or analysis.
4. Describe or refer to figures/tables where useful.
5. Refer to existing methods to compare, adapt, or justify choices.
6. Provide background facts when needed to justify decisions.
7. Identify issues, constraints, or unavoidable problems without over-apology.

Checks:

- Include enough detail for reproducibility.
- Do not hide method limitations if they affect trust.
- Use past tense for what was done, unless describing a method or system that
  generally operates in the present.

## Results

Function: report what happened and guide readers through evidence.

Generic model:

1. Revisit aim, prediction, hypothesis, gap, or method as needed.
2. Make a general statement about results.
3. Invite the reader to view a figure/table and state what it contains.
4. Present specific/key results with appropriate evaluative comments.
5. Compare with other studies, models, simulations, or predictions.
6. Explain results using known facts or method details when needed.
7. Identify problems or issues with results and their reasons.
8. State possible implications cautiously when appropriate.

Checks:

- Results should not become a full Discussion.
- Avoid naked numbers. Tell readers what the numbers mean in context.
- Use present tense for what a graphic shows; use past tense for what was done
  or found; use present tense cautiously when treating a finding as durable.

## Discussion

Function: explain what the results mean, how they fit knowledge, and where the
claim stops.

Generic model:

1. Optionally announce the structure or focus of the Discussion.
2. State the achievement or contribution of the study.
3. Reboot readers with relevant background or literature.
4. Revisit gap, aim, method, or key results.
5. Explore implications of results.
6. Map results to literature or knowledge for comparison/support.
7. Identify limitations and future work.
8. Restate achievement, contribution, or impact.
9. Identify potential applications.

Checks:

- Results = what was observed. Discussion = how to understand it and when the
  interpretation may fail.
- Use modal verbs and hedging when moving from data to interpretation.
- Do not claim universality unless the evidence supports it.

## Conclusion

Function: deliver the final take-home message for readers who may not have read
the whole paper.

Possible components:

1. What is in the paper.
2. What the paper or study achieved.
3. Relevant background information.
4. Gap, aim, or need for the study.
5. Method or approach.
6. Key results with evaluative comments.
7. Implications of the results.
8. Limitations.
9. Applications.
10. How the study advances knowledge.
11. Future directions.

Checks:

- The conclusion is not a copied abstract.
- Do not introduce new data.
- Make the contribution explicit and bounded.

## Whole-Paper Writing Order

IMRaD is usually the reading/order-of-print structure, not the best writing order.
For many research papers, a practical writing order is:

1. Methods
2. Results
3. Discussion/Conclusion
4. Introduction
5. Abstract
6. Title

Adapt for methods papers, review articles, thesis chapters, and journal rules.
