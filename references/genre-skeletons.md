# Genre Skeletons

Generic, provisional skeletons for STEMM genres beyond the standard research
article. Always reverse-engineer 2–4 real target examples of the same genre and
venue before trusting these. The standard IMRaD article model lives in
`section-models.md`; this file covers the common variants.

## Review article

Contribution = synthesis and framing of a field, not new data.

1. Scope and importance of the field; why a review now.
2. Organizing framework (thematic, chronological, or methodological) — state it explicitly.
3. Synthesize sub-themes with critical comparison, not a list of summaries.
4. Open problems, controversies, and contradictions in the literature.
5. Future directions and what the field needs next.

Risks: summarizing instead of synthesizing; claims about "the field" under-cited;
no explicit organizing logic.

## Methods / protocol paper

Contribution = the method itself; the test is reproducibility and advantage.

1. Problem the method solves and the limits of existing methods.
2. Overview and novelty of the proposed method.
3. Step-by-step, reproducible procedure (parameters, controls, versions).
4. Validation: benchmark against existing methods or ground truth.
5. Scope, failure modes, and limitations of use.
6. Availability of code, data, or materials.

Risks: Results read as discovery rather than validation; missing reproducibility
detail; tense confusion (the method *does*; the test *was done*).

## Short communication / letter

Driver = speed and a single high-value result.

1. Compressed introduction: gap and aim in a few sentences.
2. Minimal but sufficient method.
3. The key result(s), with figures carrying most of the weight.
4. Brief implication and why it matters now.

Risks: more than one main claim; length over limit; overpromising to win attention.

## Thesis-paper chapter

Reader = examiners; more signposting and explicit positioning than a journal paper.

1. Chapter aim and its link to the thesis argument.
2. Fuller literature and positioning than a paper allows.
3. Method, with more justification of choices.
4. Results and discussion.
5. Chapter conclusion that links forward to the next chapter.

Risks: too little linking between chapters; under-justified methods; a published
paper pasted in without examiner-facing framing.

## Conference abstract

Standalone; sometimes written before final results.

1. Context and importance (brief).
2. Gap and aim.
3. Approach or method.
4. Results or expected results, stated honestly.
5. Significance for the audience.

Risks: promising results not yet obtained; too dense for an oral/poster audience;
unclear contribution.

## Transfer rule

Pick the closest genre skeleton, then overwrite it with the function order, length,
and conventions found in real target examples. Mark any unverified part as provisional.
