# Language Risk Checklist

Use after structure and claim logic are sound. Do not use sentence polishing to
hide missing evidence, missing contribution, or misplaced section functions.

## Organization

- List achievements in order of importance.
- Identify contribution to knowledge, research, and real-world use where relevant.
- Make every paragraph serve one clear function.
- Start paragraphs with narrative entry sentences when helpful.
- Delete loose sentences unless they can be integrated into the paragraph's
  function.
- Keep subtitles accurate and use their keywords early in the subsection.

## Sentence Flow

- Put known information before new information when possible.
- Start sentences with overlap, `This/These + noun`, or a clear signal.
- Use connectors only when the logical relation is real.
- Avoid long jumps from general context to technical detail.
- Add comments such as `suggesting that`, `indicating that`, or `which means`
  when readers might ask "so what?"

## Sentence Length and Density

- Treat 20-26 words as a common target range, not a hard rule.
- Watch consecutive long sentences.
- Risk markers: multiple `and`, multiple `which`, many nouns, many prepositions,
  delayed main verb, and packed noun phrases.
- Break over-dense sentences into linked sentences.

## Grammar as Meaning

Verb tense:

- Past simple reports what the study found or did.
- Present simple can present a finding as durable or generally true; use it only
  when the evidence supports that level of commitment.
- Present perfect often maps past work or accumulated knowledge to the present.
- If tense changes, the reason should be clear to the reader.

Passive and subjects:

- Agentless passive can hide who did the work.
- Non-human subjects can obscure ownership of the contribution.
- Use `we`, `this study`, `our model`, `previous work`, or precise agents when
  ownership matters.

Articles:

- `a/an` and `the` can both be grammatical but mean different things.
- Use `the` for shared/specified knowledge.
- Check field-specific countability.

Prepositions:

- Prepositions can change claims: evidence of/for, improved by/up to, in/for.
- Replace ambiguous `with` when it means using, having, together with, because of,
  or at the same time as.
- Avoid strings of prepositional phrases.
- Place `for the first time` so the novelty target is unmistakable.

Reference:

- Prefer `this/these + noun` at sentence starts.
- Replace `it`, `which`, `this`, or `that` when the referent has more than one
  possible source.
- Place citations where they identify who did what.

Modal verbs and certainty:

- Match certainty to evidence.
- Use `may`, `might`, `could`, `suggest`, `indicate`, `are consistent with`, or
  `raise the possibility that` when interpreting beyond direct observation.
- Do not use risk-reducing language to weaken a well-supported central result.

Vocabulary:

- Repeat stable terms. Do not thesaurus-hop between method, framework, approach,
  tool, model, strategy, and technique unless the distinction is real.
- Use current terms from target articles.
- Check commonly confused pairs: affect/effect, imply/infer, effective/efficient,
  criterion/criteria, phenomenon/phenomena, e.g./i.e.

Punctuation:

- Overused parentheses, dash pairs, comma chains, and semicolons often signal
  overloaded sentence structure.
- If parenthetical content matters, integrate it. If not, delete it.
