# Title and Abstract Checks

## Abstract

Function: a high-stakes standalone document that lets readers and editors quickly
understand relevance, method, result, contribution, and boundary.

Generic model:

1. Significance of the topic or essential factual background.
2. Challenge, problem, gap, or need.
3. What the paper/study does.
4. Method or materials.
5. Results, including comparisons when useful.
6. Implications.
7. Mapping to existing knowledge.
8. Achievement, value, contribution, or applications.

Checks:

- The abstract derives from the paper, not the other way around.
- The word limit is not a target. Prefer clear, dense, accurate prose.
- Avoid citations unless the paper directly depends on or overturns a specific
  work and journal practice allows it.
- Avoid acronym-heavy abstracts. Define only necessary acronyms.
- Repeat key nouns when needed to remove ambiguity.
- Make the contribution impossible to miss but not stronger than the evidence.
- Check consistency against the body, especially Results and Conclusion.

Tense guidance:

- Present simple: what the paper does or contains.
- Past simple: what was done or used.
- Past or present: results and implications, depending on permanence and target
  journal convention.
- Present perfect or present simple: achievement, value, or contribution.

## Title

Function: the entry point for title-scanning readers and search systems.

Build the title after the study's contribution and conclusion are clear.

Seven checks:

1. Average length in target articles.
2. Acronym acceptability for current and future readers.
3. Title keywords compared with keyword list.
4. Grammar, especially noun stacks and prepositions.
5. Structural content in target titles: colon, method, application, contribution,
   sentence title, named tool, or field focus.
6. Expectations created by the title.
7. Whether the paper fulfills those expectations.

Risk checks:

- Does the title name only the research activity rather than the outcome?
- Does it overload nouns or compound nouns?
- Does it contain ambiguous prepositions?
- Does the keyword list complement rather than duplicate the title?
- Does it overpromise novelty, mechanism, clinical value, application, or
  generality?

## Highlights, Significance Statements, and Graphical Abstracts

Treat these as separate genres. Reverse engineer current examples from the target
journal before drafting.

Check:

- what readers can understand without the full article
- how much technical detail is normal
- whether the contribution is explicit
- whether claims are consistent with the abstract and conclusion
